## Correctness verdict

**Part 1: FIX, not survive. Confidence: high.**

The core inference in the draft’s proof is not valid at the stated level of generality. The draft argues that if a separating datum is a function of the whole state but not of the `R`-part, then it must be a nonconstant function of the complement. That is true for a purely classical ontic model whose full configuration literally decomposes as a point `(r, r^c)`. It is not true for quantum states or statistical states, where the difference can live in **joint correlations** rather than in the complement alone. The literature is explicit that some information can be hidden in correlations while being inaccessible from either subsystem alone: restricted sets of quantum states can be “masked,” with the hidden information retrievable only from joint correlation measurements, not from either marginal by itself. citeturn24view0turn24view1turn31academia0

That means the current statement “the separating datum is a function of the complement” is false unless the model class is narrowed. There is, however, a nearby statement that is likely correct and useful: the separating datum lies **outside the sigma-algebra / observable algebra generated by `M_R`**, and may require access to global degrees of freedom, relational observables, or `R`–`R^c` correlations. Koashi and Imoto give a structurally similar decomposition result: for preserving a known state ensemble, the “nonclassical part is inaccessible” and the classical part is “read-only.” Fawzi and Renner likewise formalize when a global tripartite state is reconstructible from a marginal and when it is not. Those results show the right language is algebraic and reconstructive, not “function of the complement” simpliciter. citeturn23view2turn23view3

There is also a second subtlety. Braunstein and Pati’s no-hiding theorem supports a **special case** close to what the draft wants: if information is completely bleached from a subsystem, then it moves to the environment and not to correlations. But that theorem is not your Part 1 as stated; it is a theorem about complete hiding of **arbitrary** quantum states under a specific kind of process. The masking literature shows the opposite behavior for restricted state sets. So the literature does **not** support the draft’s unrestricted complement-function conclusion; it supports a much more conditional split between no-hiding and masking regimes. citeturn24view2turn24view3turn24view0turn31academia0

**Verdict on Part 1:** the present proof is broken as written, but the underlying idea is salvageable if you either restrict to classical finite-state ontic configurations or restate the lemma in algebraic/global-correlation language.

**Part 2: BROKEN as stated. Confidence: high on the break; medium-high on the repair path.**

The strongest attack succeeds: conservation laws and superselection/resource-theoretic symmetry restrictions give you a genuine candidate **fourth mode** that is neither a stipulated budget gap nor an asymptotic hardness claim. The Wigner–Araki–Yanase line of work states that additive conservation laws constrain exact measurements; Kuramochi and Tajima sharpen this to the statement that additive conservation laws imply commutativity of exactly implementable projective measurements with the conserved observable, and that exact position measurement under momentum conservation is impossible under the Yanase condition. They also show analogous restrictions for exact implementation of unitary channels under conservation laws. That is an exact, single-instance, physically forced barrier. citeturn25view0turn23view6turn35academia0

You can try to absorb this by saying the forbidden operation is simply outside the menu, hence “declared.” But that move changes the theorem’s subject. The draft explicitly wants to distinguish declared restrictions from physically forced ones, where the inability follows from “dynamics/structure, not a stipulated access-restriction.” A symmetry-forbidden measurement or unitary is exactly such a structurally forced inability. If your theorem excludes it by definition, then the theorem has been narrowed by fiat away from the most important physical counterexamples. If it does not exclude it, then E0/E1/E2 are not exhaustive. citeturn25view0turn23view6turn35academia0

The reference-frame and superselection literature makes the picture more nuanced, not less damaging. Bartlett, Rudolph, and Spekkens emphasize that lack of a shared reference frame induces operational restrictions that appear as superselection rules, and that relational encodings can sometimes contend with those restrictions. This means some apparent superselection barriers are indeed “declared-like” in the sense that omitted relational resources matter. But the same literature is resource-theoretic: those resources are not automatically free, and the WAY literature shows that exact implementation can remain impossible without the right asymmetry resource. So the best charitable reading is not “Part 2 survives,” but rather: **you have conflated at least two different mechanisms—mere omission of a reference structure, and exact symmetry no-go under admissible dynamics.** citeturn23view0turn23view1turn20academia3turn20academia2turn25view0

There is a separate, independent problem with the draft’s “single-instance cost is always finite because one can brute-force enumerate the configuration.” In one-shot quantum settings that is not an operationally legitimate primitive. An arbitrary quantum state cannot in general be exactly converted into classical bits from a single specimen, and the accessible classical information from a quantum system is fundamentally limited. So “the configuration is finite, therefore brute-force extraction is finite” is fine for explicit classical states in a finite table, but not for a generic finite-dimensional quantum model. That objection does not even need conservation laws; it already blocks the closure argument in the quantum reading of the theorem. citeturn31search9turn19search9

**Verdict on Part 2:** broken as a universal claim. To rescue anything theorem-like, you must either narrow the scope sharply or add a new structural-obstruction case.

## Strongest single objection

The single most damaging objection is the **WAY / superselection / symmetry-resource objection**: exact operations can be forbidden in a finite, closed, single-instance quantum model for structural reasons that are not merely large-but-finite extraction cost and are not merely conditional computational hardness. In the modern formulation, exact measurement of an asymmetric observable via symmetric processing is impossible unless one has access to a perfectly asymmetric resource, and exact implementations of certain measurements and channels under conservation laws are impossible. citeturn23view6turn25view0turn35academia0

As the theorem is currently written, that objection is fatal to the exhaustiveness claim. The cleanest repair is one of these two paths.

The first path is **scope restriction**: explicitly say the theorem does not address physically admissible-operation constraints arising from symmetry, superselection, or conservation laws. On this reading, your theorem becomes a restricted claim about boundaries produced by limited access to co-present information in a model where enlarged admissible operations are otherwise available. That saves a narrower result, but it is a major retreat.

The second path is **taxonomy repair**: add an **E3 structural symmetry obstruction** category, separate from E0/E1/E2. If you want to keep a unified resource language, you could further say that some exact symmetry-restricted tasks require an infinite implementation resource in the relevant resource theory; recent generalized WAY work explicitly proves universal resource-cost / irreversibility tradeoffs and shows that some resource-non-increasing operations require infinite implementation cost. But that is not your current theorem; it is a different one, and it still requires serious case-splitting between approximate implementability, exact implementability, and omitted reference-frame resources. citeturn18academia0turn25view0

My bottom line is blunt: **the result does not survive this objection in its present form.** It needs a real fix, not a footnote.

## Publishability and venues

**Publishability verdict: not publishable as a hard-theorem paper in its current form. Confidence: high.**

If you submitted this as a theorem paper now, a competent referee could reject it on two short grounds. First, Part 1 is not proved at the claimed generality because the proof fails in quantum/statistical models with correlation-only separation. Second, Part 2’s exhaustiveness claim is false as stated because the WAY/symmetry literature gives a serious finite single-instance structural obstruction class. Those are not cosmetic issues; they hit the main statements. citeturn24view0turn25view0turn23view6

There is still a publishable paper nearby, but it is probably **not** the paper you currently have in mind.

If you **narrow to a classical finite-state ontic setting** and prove a genuinely rigorous algebraic result about access-generated equivalence classes, then you could have a small but real theorem paper, probably aimed at a foundations/TCS crossover audience.

If you **keep the quantum/foundational ambition**, the better target is a **conceptual foundations paper** rather than a theorem paper. In that version, the main contribution would be a clarified taxonomy of capability boundaries, explicitly situated relative to idealization/limit literature, cryptographic indistinguishability, and symmetry-resource no-go theorems. But that version must present itself as a synthesis and taxonomy paper with carefully bounded claims, not as an already-general no-go theorem.

Plausible venues after major repair would be journals such as **Foundations of Physics**, **Studies in History and Philosophy of Modern Physics**, **Synthese**, or **Philosophy of Science**. A more formal, narrower CS-flavored version might fit a logic/TCS or quantum-information foundations venue, but only after the theorem is cut down to something actually proved.
