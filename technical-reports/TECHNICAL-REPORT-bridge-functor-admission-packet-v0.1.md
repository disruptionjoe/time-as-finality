## Core Reframe

The old local-minimum pattern was:

```text
candidate analogy -> one absorber fires -> candidate demotes
```

The improved pattern is:

```text
candidate phenomenon -> declared absorber stack -> one of three outcomes
```

Those outcomes are:

1. **Single-absorber demotion**: one known absorber fully explains the effect.
2. **Composite absorber explanation**: no single absorber is the right account,
   but a declared stack of known absorbers explains the effect without new
   TaF structure.
3. **Composite residual**: after the declared stack is granted, a capability
   spread or obstruction remains. This is the only route to a new review target.

The second outcome matters. It prevents the repo from treating every failed
novelty attempt as sterile. Sometimes the research gain is learning that a
previously confusing phenomenon is best understood as the composition of
ordinary components: full-history access plus provenance completion, or
competency profile plus resource preorder, or observer budget plus retrieval
workload.

## Admission Packet

A Bridge-Functor Admission Packet is a preclaim intake form for any proposed
reopening of a closed route.

Minimum fields:

```text
Y       source structure
A       admissible source states, A subset Y
O       observer/access profile
pi      visible projection, pi : A -> X
T       task family
h       horizon
B       boundary, budget, permission, or resource condition
Cap     native capability object, Cap : A -> K
R_K     native comparison on K
S       ordered absorber stack
L       domain-native law, theorem, cost, measure, morphism, or protocol
D       demotion path
```

The packet asks:

```text
After granting the declared absorber stack S, does pi still fail to determine
Cap under the native comparison R_K for the declared task family T?
```

If yes, the packet may define a new review target.

If no, the packet must still record whether the demotion was a single-absorber
demotion or a composite absorber explanation.

## Absorber Stack

An absorber stack is an ordered list of ordinary explanatory completions,
equivalences, profiles, or state enrichments that the candidate agrees to grant
before claiming residue.

Common absorbers:

- full source state;
- full visible history;
- full provenance/log/ledger/server state;
- full competency/task-success profile;
- resource preorder or monotone vector;
- ordinary transition-system completion;
- boundary enlargement or permission completion;
- law-sector completion;
- gauge, coordinate, basis, or relabeling quotient;
- statistical uncertainty or tolerance envelope;
- native sufficient statistic, belief state, or process equivalence.

The stack is ordered because some absorbers can be order-sensitive. For example,
quotienting by relabeling before adding provenance may differ from adding
provenance before quotienting. That order-sensitivity is not automatically
novel. It is a signal to make the stack explicit.

## Composite Effects

A candidate may earn interest without earning novelty if it identifies a
composite effect.

Composite effect:

```text
A phenomenon P appears surprising under one impoverished projection, but is
explained by the interaction of two or more known absorbers under a declared
stack S.
```

Examples of plausible composite-effect shapes:

- **bounded retrieval effect**: observer budget plus retained-state summary
  explains why native retained tasks pass while arbitrary retrieval fails;
- **authoritative commit effect**: local record plus server/ledger/provenance
  completion explains apparent finality without new finality structure;
- **competency-resource effect**: task-success profile plus resource preorder
  explains a C(R)-like capability split;
- **typed-translation effect**: schema-preserving translation plus object
  identity rejection explains why two gaps share interface but not objecthood;
- **scale bookkeeping effect**: reachability quotient plus refinement naturalness
  explains role persistence without clock, duration, or scale genesis.

These are legitimate research outputs when recorded as explanations rather
than promotions.

## Composite Residual

A composite residual is stronger:

```text
After every absorber in S is granted in its native form, there remains a
non-singleton capability spread, invariant obstruction, lower-bound gap, or
preservation failure that cannot be removed without adding a nontrivial new
object.
```

A composite residual must satisfy all of the following:

1. `Cap` and `R_K` are declared before pair selection.
2. The absorber stack `S` is declared before pair selection.
3. The domain-native law `L` is not invented from the witness pair.
4. Positive controls pass under the same `R_K`.
5. Negative controls fail under the same `R_K`.
6. The minimal enrichment is smaller than `(pi, Cap)` as a trivial dashboard.
7. The result has an explicit demotion path.

If any condition fails, the result can still be a composite absorber
explanation, but not a residual.

## Bridge-Functor Language

The word "bridge" is deliberately weak. It means an admissible translation
between audit surfaces, not identity and not support from a neighboring theory.

The word "functor" is also conditional. A packet may use functor language only
when it declares:

```text
source category or structured class
target category or structured class
objects
morphisms
identity preservation
composition preservation
native comparison R_K
absorber stack S
```

If composition or identity is not proved, the packet is a bridge schema or
adapter, not a functor.

Use these labels in future reports and gates:

## Operational Rule

Future work should stop asking:

```text
Can this analogy survive every absorber?
```

and instead ask:

```text
What does the full absorber stack explain, and is there any residual after the
stack is granted in its strongest native form?
```

This makes negative results more productive. A failed novelty route can still
advance the research by producing a compact explanation of a previously
confusing effect as a stack of known components.
