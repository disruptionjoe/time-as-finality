# Technical Report: Q1A Accessible-Class Sufficiency v0.1

## Claim Under Test

T103 found a fixed-data internal Q1A witness. T104 then absorbed that witness
into provenance-aware Quantum Darwinism once both sides shared the same
audited fragment partition. T105 asks what verdict structure still survives in
the current fixed-data Q1A branch after that absorption.

## Artifact

T105 exhausts the finite witness family generated by:

- all access cuts over three informative fragments `E1`, `E2`, `E3`;
- all provenance partitions of those three fragments;
- one hidden-partition control.

Across that family it compares:

- raw accessible redundancy;
- audited accessible provenance-class support;
- the D1 verdict;
- a reduced support-classifier verdict using only
  `accessible_provenance_support >= threshold`.

## Result

The surviving fixed-data Q1A branch collapses to a sufficient statistic:

```text
D1 verdict = withhold      if the provenance partition is unavailable
          = finalized     if accessible provenance support >= 3
          = not_finalized otherwise
```

Within this family, once the ordinary quantum-side summaries are fixed,
partition geometry, fragment labels, and raw accessible redundancy do not add
verdict content beyond the audited accessible provenance-class count.

Raw redundancy is still not sufficient. Cases with raw accessible redundancy
`3` split between `finalized` and `not_finalized` depending on whether the
audited partition yields support `3` or `2`.

## Current Strongest Claim

Q1A should currently be read as audited accessible-class bookkeeping over
already formed records. In the tested fixed-data regime it is not a distinct
measurement theory, and it is not even a rich structural rule: it is a
thresholded classifier over accessible provenance support plus a partition
visibility check.

## What This Improved

T105 gives the project a much sharper falsification target. A future Q1A
upgrade can no longer rely on generic talk about observer-relative access or
partition structure. It must show one of two things:

- two fixed-data cases with the same audited accessible provenance support but
  different D1 verdicts; or
- a physically justified D1 dimension beyond accessible provenance support that
  remains load-bearing after the provenance audit is shared.

That is a better research standard than continuing to accumulate nearby
bookkeeping examples.

## What This Weakened Or Falsified

This weakens Q1A again. The surviving branch is narrower than "access-boundary
record accounting" suggests if that phrase is read as rich new measurement
structure. In the present family, the verdict content reduces to an audited
counting rule.

This does not kill Q1A entirely. It kills the stronger reading that the
current fixed-data evidence already points to a nontrivial observer-relative
measurement mechanism.

## Falsification Condition

T105 stands unless a future fixed-data witness keeps the ordinary quantum-side
summaries fixed and still forces different D1 verdicts at the same audited
accessible provenance-support count, or unless branch support, reversal cost,
or another physically justified D1 dimension becomes genuinely load-bearing.

## Claim Ledger Update

Add T105 to Q1A:

```text
In the current fixed-data witness family, D1 verdicts are completely
determined by the audited accessible provenance-class count plus partition
visibility. This narrows Q1A from a general observer-relative measurement claim
to a thresholded record-accounting classifier.
```

## Open Blocker

No current Q1A witness escapes the accessible-class reduction after the
provenance audit is shared. The repo still lacks a branch-support,
reversal-cost, or other physically justified witness that remains nontrivial
under that reduction.

## Next Work

The next high-value move is to try to break this reduction, not to decorate
it. The best target is a fixed-data witness where branch support or
reversal-cost content survives after matching accessible provenance support.
If that fails, Q1A should be demoted again in paper-facing summaries.
